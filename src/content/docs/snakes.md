## An Introduction to Snakes

Snakes are fascinating reptiles famous for their elongated, legless bodies and remarkable adaptability. They are found all over the world, except in Antarctica, Iceland, Ireland, and New Zealand. They belong to the suborder **Serpentes** within the class **Reptilia**. 

Notably, snakes are ectothermic, or "cold-blooded," meaning their body temperature varies with their environment. This makes them highly dependent on external conditions for activity and survival.

## Types of Snakes

There are over 3,000 species of snakes, broadly categorized into venomous and non-venomous.

### Venomous Snakes
Venomous snakes include cobras, vipers, and rattlesnakes. Their venom helps them take down prey quickly and begins the digestion process before the food is swallowed. Venoms differ in composition from neurotoxins (affecting the nervous system) to hemotoxins (affecting blood and tissue).

### Non-Venomous Snakes
Non-venomous snakes like pythons and garter snakes rely on constriction or simply swallowing their prey whole. Famous examples include the ball python, which is a popular pet species, and boas, which are known for their impressive strength and size.

## Snake Habitat

Snakes thrive in a diverse range of habitats—forests, deserts, wetlands, grasslands, and even urban areas. Their adaptability to various climates helps maintain a natural balance. Snakes often use camouflage in their environment to hide from predators or ambush prey, making them masters of stealth and survival.

## Diet

Most snakes are carnivorous. Depending on the species and size, they feed on rodents, birds, eggs, frogs, insects, and even fish. Larger species, such as anacondas, have been known to prey on deer, capybaras, and other large mammals.
